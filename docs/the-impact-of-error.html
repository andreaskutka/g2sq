Chapter 3 The Impact of Error

Sample surveys aggregate responses collected from individual units to obtain statistics for the sample, often with the aim to infer corresponding population values, referred to as population parameters.

In survey context, error refers to the difference between an observed value and the underlying true value. Error from any source can be either random or systematic, and uncorrelated or correlated, with different implications on statistical analysis.

Error would be considered random if it does not follow any pattern. For example, if respondents have difficulty recalling an item, some respondents might give higher values while others provide lower values. Across all observations, the error would have a mean of zero and would not affect the mean value of the variable. However, random error increases the variance of a variable, which in turn reduces the reliability or precision of a measure. The higher the variance, the higher the probability that the measure would be different if the survey were to be repeated under the exact same conditions. The results would be less reliable. Increased variance furthermore reduces the magnitude of correlations with other variables and the statistical power in hypothesis tests.

Error is systematic if it contains a systematic tendency. It is usually referred to as bias. As an example, if interviewers tend to not report all household members in order to shorten interview duration, the survey estimate of household size will underestimate the actual household size in the population. In statistical terms, the sample mean would be a negatively biased estimator of the population mean. Since systematic error directly affects the mean of a variable, it reduces the validity of the measure, in other words the measure is not accurately measuring what it is supposed to. If the systematic error is not constant, it may furthermore increase the variance of the variable, with the effects described above.

Another key distinction is between uncorrelated and correlated error. Error is uncorrelated if the error for different units (e.g. households) is unrelated. An example would be occasionally typos made by interviewers when recording answer options that do not follow any particular pattern. Above discussion on the effects of random and systematic error was based on uncorrelated error. As we saw, the increased variance and bias can have serious effects on statistics.

If the error for different units is related, we speak of correlated error. For example, interviews conducted by one interviewer who has misunderstood a question and administered it wrongly will contain the same error, while those of another interviewer may not. Correlated error has much more sever effects on statistics as it multiplies the variance. Biemer and Lyberg (2003) show that only with a moderate intra-interviewer correlation coefficient of \(\rho_{int} = 0.03\), the interviewer design effect of \(def_{int}\) may be as large as 2.47, or in other words that the variance of a variable is increased by 1.5 times due to the interviewer error.

3.1 Variable error

Can occur at any point in the survey process (design, sampling, training, fieldwork, processing, etc.) and from any unit (researcher, interviewer, respondent, etc.)

Validity could be violated through misspecification/method of collection as well as other errors in the implementation process

A lot of effort/interest/focus on sampling error and design, but often very little on non-sampling error

3.2 Interviewer and team effects

In survey that are assisted by interviewers, either in person or over the phone, error between individual units might be correlated if conducted by the same interviewer. Some interviewers, by their level of understanding, interaction with the respondents, level of motivation and other characteristics may produce responses that are more positive (or negative) than those of other interviewers. Interviewer error is systematic within the interviews conducted by one interviewer, but variable across the assignments. They are referred to as intra-interviewer correlated error or interviewer effect.

Error might also be correlated for other survey roles, including supervisors, data monitors, editors or keyers. As an example, one data monitor might thoroughly review certain items and provide good feedback to their assigned interviewers, reducing measurement error within their team.
