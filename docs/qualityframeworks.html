Chapter 4 Total Survey Quality

early survey researchers equated small survey error with survey usefulness or survey quality. extended frameworks

“The TSE paradigm is part of the much broader concept of total survey quality, which considers the fitness for use of an estimate. The ‘‘fitness for use’’ concept (Juran and Gryna 1980) recognizes that producers and users of survey data often perceive survey quality from very different perspectives.”

quality dimensions differ between surveys.

multitude of surveys out there and different users, so user needs will differ

how can a survey yield useful results?

non-statistical quality components

Most national statistical offices (NSOs) in developed countries have adopted survey quality frameworks that include a subset of the quality dimensions summarized in Table 4.1. (Biemer 2010)

there are other ways to TSE to think about information quality

Table 4.1: Common Dimensions of a Survey Quality Framework.
Dimension Description
Accuracy Total survey error is minimized
Credibility Data are considered trustworthy by the survey community
Comparability Demographic, spatial, and temporal comparisons are valid
Usability/Interpretability Documentation is clear and metadata are well-managed
Relevance Data satisfy users needs
Accessibility Access to the data is user friendly
Timeliness/Punctuality Data deliveries adhere to schedules
Completeness Data are rich enough to satisfy the analysis objectives without undue burden on respondents
Coherence Estimates from different sources can be reliably combined

some of the dimensions can be in conflict with each other, e.g. producing rich and wealth documentation to satisfy usability might affect timeliness of data.

not easily quantifiable.

4.1 Maximising Quality in Surveys

  • there are constraint, budget time within the survey budget and time constraints and relevant ethical concerns.

  • can also think of other quality dimensions as constraints, such as comparability.

  • can’t reduce every erro source.

  • TSE paradigm see the minimization

  • look at it holistically, and reduce total survey error within the constraints. example, increasing sample size may reduce sampling error, but will increase measurement error.

Minimize the total survey error (TSE). For example, while increasing the sample size can reduce the sampling error, it might require more interviewers to be trained and a higher volume of interviews per day to be monitored, increasing other non-sampling error types that quickly outweigh the improved sampling error.

As we will see in later chapters, reducing sample error (e.g. by increasing the sample size) can increase error from other sources and might not be good overall strategy to increase the quality or precision of our estimates.

  • define the quality indicators that matter to the survey

  • identify potential sources of error

, which many consider the conceptual foundation of the field of survey methodology. useful tool to focus

In addition to these typologies serving as checklists of possible errors and their estimation, it is important to find ways to eliminate their root causes.

while not it is not easy to quantity error other than sampling error, and it is usually not done in the survey community, the TSE still is a useful framework to conceptualize error, identify the biggest sources and inform design decisions. isolate specific error sources and identify means the reduce or eliminate them.

In order to identify the largest error sources and inform design decisions,

  • follow a strategy like Biemer 2010, suggest to maximise accuracy while ensuring other user-centric quality components are also met.

The aim is not to try to eliminate error from all survey stages. Even if it were feasible, it would certainly bust any survey’s budget and time constraints. Instead, surveys should strive to maximise total survey quality within the survey constriants.

In reality this is often interactive and reacting to the circumstances and the developments in the survey. See Biemer 2010 A proactive, dynamic, flexible approach to survey implementation

real time monitoring, Minimizing the TSE within the resource and time constraints requires to be efficient …
